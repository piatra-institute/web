# Scarcity Over Technology: What Abundance Cannot Buy

## Abstract

Technology keeps making things cheaper, so why does scarcity persist? This
playground models scarcity as the shadow price on a binding constraint and asks a
pointed question: which scarcities does technology dissolve, and which does it
leave untouched? The answer turns on what is actually binding. When it is
material capacity, more technology helps. When it is something technology cannot
multiply, attention, time, care, or an artificially maintained limit, abundance
hits a wall.

## Scarcity as a shadow price

In constrained optimization, every binding constraint has a dual multiplier: the
amount by which the objective would improve if the constraint were relaxed by one
unit. That multiplier is the natural definition of scarcity, the shadow price.
When a constraint stops binding, its multiplier drops to zero and the resource is
effectively free, like air.

The model makes this concrete. With N users sharing a binding capacity, scarcity
is N divided by that capacity, the per-user shortfall. The calibration panel pins
it: 200 users on a capacity of 1000 give a scarcity of 0.2.

## Technology versus the constraint

Technology T produces capacity as a power law, (1 minus friction) times a
coefficient times T to the efficacy exponent k, with corruption skimming off
another fixed fraction. Raise T and material capacity rises, so material scarcity
falls. This is the optimistic story: the calibration confirms linear-tech capacity
of 100 times T, halved by 50% friction.

But the model adds a second constraint: an attention ceiling A that caps usable
capacity no matter how much is produced. Once that ceiling binds, scarcity equals
users over the ceiling, and it no longer depends on technology at all. The
calibration drives this home: with the ceiling binding, multiplying technology
ten-fold changes scarcity by exactly zero. Attention, time, and care are
resources technology cannot manufacture, and an artificially maintained scarcity
is a political fact, not a technical one.

## The welfare reading

Welfare in the model is a log-utility sum, N times the log of the per-capita
allocation. Log utility means proportional gains matter equally and welfare
collapses toward minus infinity as the per-capita share approaches zero. So the
model can show technology raising welfare in the material regime while being
powerless against it in the attention-bound regime, the same intervention with
opposite verdicts depending on what binds.

## Honest limits

This is a stylized argument, not an empirical model. The power-law capacity,
log utility, and fixed friction and corruption are simplifying choices; the
parameters are illustrative. Friction and corruption are exogenous skims that do
not respond to the value of the resource, which real corruption certainly does.
The point is conceptual: to separate scarcities technology can engineer away from
those it cannot, and to name the latter as choices rather than constraints.

# The Algebra of Conscience: Two Ways to Be Good

## Abstract

Can morality be done with algebra? Vladimir Lefebvre thought so. His theory of
reflexive control claims that human ethical cognition runs on one of two formal
systems, distinguished not by which acts they call good but by how good and evil
combine. From that single axiom, four moral archetypes fall out. This playground
embodies the theory as interacting agents, and the algebra underneath is exact,
which the calibration pins.

## Two ethical systems

The crux is composition. In **System 1**, which Lefebvre associated (controversially)
with Western liberal culture, compromise with good is good and compromise with
evil is evil; conflict is the suspect mode. In **System 2**, the polarity flips:
compromise of any kind is corrupting, and uncompromising confrontation is the
virtuous stance. Same acts, opposite valuations of the act of compromising.

This is not a metaphor in the model, it is a sign flip in a Boolean algebra. The
playground encodes the consequence directly: the tendency (compromise or conflict)
that counts as virtuous reverses between the two systems.

## Four archetypes

Cross two binary attitudes, self-evaluation (high or low) and behavioural tendency
(compromise or conflict), and you get four moral characters:

- **Saint**: low self-evaluation, the virtuous tendency for the system.
- **Hero**: high self-evaluation, the virtuous tendency.
- **Opportunist**: low self-evaluation, the non-virtuous tendency.
- **Hypocrite**: high self-evaluation, the non-virtuous tendency.

Self-evaluation is intrinsic to the archetype: hero and hypocrite think well of
themselves, saint and opportunist do not. But the tendency depends on the system.
The calibration checks both axes: the hero's high self-image, the saint's low one,
and the decisive flip, the saint compromises in System 1 but conflicts in System
2, while the opportunist takes the opposite tendency.

## From algebra to agents

The playground sets these archetypes loose as agents that help and harm their
neighbours, with costs, benefits, in-group factors, and a growing awareness that
adjusts behaviour. Watching them, you can see how a population's moral makeup and
its governing ethical system shape who thrives and how cooperation or conflict
spreads.

That agent layer is a stylized illustration, not part of Lefebvre's formal
algebra, and the assumptions panel says so. The resource dynamics are modelling
choices; the simulation is stochastic and varies run to run. What is exact and
checkable is the archetype algebra itself.

## Honest scope

Lefebvre's mapping of System 1 and System 2 onto real cultures (US versus Soviet
moral intuition) is his empirical claim, offered here for exploration, not
endorsed. Whether two systems exhaust human moral cognition is genuinely
contested. The model formalizes how moral stances compose, not which is right; the
exact content is the algebra, the cultural reading and the agent dynamics are the
lens.

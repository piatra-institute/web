# The Rise of the Three-Quarter Law: Body Size and Metabolism

## Abstract

An animal's resting metabolic rate scales with its body mass as a power law. The
exponent is famously close to three quarters rather than the two thirds a simple
surface-to-volume argument would suggest. This companion explains what the
playground shows, why the exponent matters, what mechanism has been proposed for
it, and why a single universal value remains contested.

## Background: a pattern across twenty orders of magnitude

Larger animals burn more energy in absolute terms but less per unit of body
mass. A mouse, gram for gram, runs far hotter than an elephant. When you plot
basal metabolic rate B against body mass M for animals spanning many orders of
magnitude and use logarithmic axes on both, the points fall along a remarkably
straight line. A straight line in log-log coordinates is the signature of a
power law:

> B = B0 * M^a, equivalently log B = log B0 + a * log M.

The slope of the line is the exponent a. That single number is the object of a
century of argument.

## Two thirds versus three quarters

The oldest prediction is geometric. Heat is lost across a surface, surface area
grows as the square of length, and volume (hence mass) grows as the cube, so
metabolic rate should scale as mass to the two-thirds power. This is the surface
law, associated with Rubner in the nineteenth century.

In 1932 Max Kleiber compiled metabolic data and found the exponent was closer to
three quarters. The result was robust enough across mammals to become known as
Kleiber's law. Quarter-power exponents then turned up in many other biological
rates and times, from lifespan to heart rate, which made a three-quarter
metabolic exponent feel like a deep regularity rather than a fluke.

The playground makes this concrete. The exponent sweep traces the fit error as
the exponent varies with the coefficient held fixed. The error minimum sits much
closer to 3/4 than to 2/3. But the valley is broad, not a sharp spike, which is
the honest visual point: the data clearly reject a very shallow or very steep
slope, while leaving real room for argument about the precise value.

## A proposed mechanism: resource networks

Why three quarters and not two thirds? The best-known modern answer is the
model of West, Brown and Enquist (1997). They argue that metabolic rate is set
by the rate at which resources can be delivered to cells through a branching,
space-filling transport network (blood vessels, airways, plant vasculature) that
has been optimised to minimise transport cost. The geometry of such an optimal,
fractal-like network produces quarter-power scaling, and a three-quarter
exponent for metabolic rate in particular.

The model is elegant and influential, and also contested. Critics point out that
the derivation makes strong idealisations, that not all organisms have such
networks, and that the empirical exponent varies between taxa.

## Why a single universal exponent is doubted

Careful reanalyses find that the best-fit exponent depends on which animals are
included, on how metabolic rate is measured, and on the statistical method.
White and Seymour, working with cleaner mammalian basal data, reported values
that some read as closer to two thirds. Glazier's metabolic-level boundaries
hypothesis argues that the exponent is not one number but varies systematically
with activity and ecology, sitting between surface-limited and volume-limited
extremes.

There is also a measurement subtlety. Basal metabolic rate is taken at rest, in
a thermoneutral environment, with no food being digested. The field metabolic
rate of a free-living animal is several times higher and scales with its own
coefficient. The conclusions here are specific to basal rate.

## What the playground is, and is not

This is a transparent sandbox for the scaling relationship, not a validated
physiological model. The ten animals are representative, order-of-magnitude
values chosen to show the pattern legibly, not a curated dataset with error
bars. The calibration panel computes the three-quarter prediction for each
animal's mass and compares it to the measured value, and is honest that the
predictions land within a few tens of percent, not exactly on the data.

The defensible takeaway is the one the error sweep shows directly: metabolic
rate scales with a power of mass whose exponent is clearly steeper than the
naive two-thirds surface law, and close to three quarters. The exact value, and
whether one universal value even exists, is where the science is still live.
